About

How I became a psychologist — and why I’ll forever be in debt to the U.S. government.

I’ve worked with the system, around it, and against it when it needed to be. Here’s the honest version of how I got here, and what I’m still fighting for.

Early life

  • Not rich, not poor.
  • Not popular or smart.
  • Not well-behaved or well-mannered…
  • Overly confident but unmotivated.
  • Boy-crazy, hard-headed, independent.
  • Self-raised since 16.
  • Left my hometown after high school.
  • College was wild, as it should be.
  • Moved to Florida to begin my career in 2007.

The path

Nova Southeastern University in Fort Lauderdale was an expensive private school, but it had the forensic psychology program I wanted. From the start, my goal was to work as a psychologist in the Federal Bureau of Prisons — with people who had committed serious violent offenses — to support reentry and help prevent future violence.

During graduate school I worked in private practice and fell in love with the independence. I worked with battered women in the Broward County Jail and with juveniles in detention — deeply meaningful work — and spent time in courtrooms watching how the legal and psychological systems overlap and shape people’s lives.

Eventually I landed my first-choice internship at the Federal Bureau of Prisons in Terre Haute, Indiana — home to the only federal death row. It was life-changing: exhilarating, thrilling, depressing, and ultimately disappointing. I witnessed significant racism within the BOP and was viewed by some staff as an “inmate-lover.” I realized it wasn’t an environment where I could thrive or create the change I believed in. After a short stint in the probation and jail department of the Houston, Texas system, I returned to private practice.

Once I became licensed in 2016, I joined Florida State Hospital in Chattahoochee, working with people found incompetent to proceed or not guilty by reason of insanity. The work was fascinating and gave me a wide range of forensic and clinical experience. After a few years, I opened my own practice and began building the career I have today.

What I’m into

I’m most energized where psychology, technology, and real life meet — virtual reality, mindfulness, biofeedback, and “vibe-coding” apps that help people understand their own minds and bodies. I care deeply about violence and gun laws, and how systems respond to risk and trauma. Clinically, I love assessments and complex cases, especially ADHD and co-occurring issues. Outside the office, I’m drawn to art, painting, and plants — and I’m passionate about the thoughtful, science-informed use of cannabis and psychedelic medicines in mental-health care.

Changes I want to see

1

Gender equality

A world where women are respected, paid, heard, and free — challenging patriarchy in courtrooms, bedrooms, and boardrooms. Every damn space.

2

Consent culture

Redefining sex, safety, and relationships through mutual respect. Consent isn’t optional — it’s the foundation. From kink to court, real power starts with “yes.”

3

Mental health & justice reform

Ending the criminalization of mental illness. Trauma-informed systems where care replaces punishment and prevention replaces reaction.

4

Creativity & self-expression

Making as medicine. Reclaiming the imagination as a tool for healing — building creative habits that move people from survival into expression, without shame and without silence.

5

Legalization & education of medicinal plants

Access to nature’s original medicine. Centering cannabis and psychedelics as tools for healing, not criminalization — deconstructing stigma with science, history, and bold public education.

“Justice that frees. Therapy that empowers. Creativity that heals.”
